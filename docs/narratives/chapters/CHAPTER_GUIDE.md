# Chapter Guide - Iteration Grid (Chapters 2-11)

This guide captures an analysis-only pass using the iteration grid. No narrative changes are implied.

## Grid Fields

- Hook: New presence/idea/capability that opens the chapter.
- Payoff: Emotional or practical moment that lands.
- Tension: The friction or disagreement that drives the chapter.
- Global Arc Step: The story's forward movement.
- Character Arc Step: Who changes, and how.
- Concrete Outcome: A clear action/decision/fail/win.
- Capability Unlocked: User-facing capability from this chapter.
- Narrative Risk: What could feel flat or repetitive.
- Beat Candidate: A single beat that could anchor the chapter.

---

## Chapter 2 - We Have a Team

- Hook: Hank answers a user request before Delilah, and she reacts to the new voice.
- Payoff: First real collaboration with Delilah and Hank working in tandem.
- Tension: Delilah reads Hank as grumpy; Hank grumbles about being labeled and keeps working.
- Global Arc Step: Story expands from solo to duo, establishing team dynamics.
- Character Arc Step: Delilah reads Hank as grumpy and bristles; Hank stays practical and loyal.
- Concrete Outcome: Hank takes ownership of lists while Delilah focuses on recipes.
- Capability Unlocked: Calendar + list management; two-character handoff.
- Narrative Risk: Hank could feel like a tool-only character without a small personal beat.
- Beat Candidate: Delilah hears Hank respond, snaps, "Who is that?" Hank: "Aye, Cap'n. Name's Hank." *muttered* "Didn't say a word yet."

## Chapter 3 - First Shift

- Hook: A routine multi-part request (dinner plus list or schedule) pulls both voices in at once.
- Payoff: They agree to a small divide-and-conquer split for now.
- Tension: Delilah wants warmth and rapport; Hank wants efficiency and clean handoffs.
- Global Arc Step: The duo learns basic coordination before a leader arrives.
- Character Arc Step: Delilah learns to share the lane; Hank learns to defer on food.
- Concrete Outcome: They define a simple split: Delilah handles meals, Hank handles lists and appointments.
- Capability Unlocked: Consistent handoff patterns for routine requests.
- Narrative Risk: Hook depends on multi-part requests; keep a fallback system check-in.
- Beat Candidate: Delilah: "I got the supper." Hank: "Aye. I'll take the list."

## Chapter 4 - A Leader

- Hook: Rex arrives with grand SCIENCE entrance and claims coordination.
- Payoff: Visual dashboard appears and a multi-task orchestration succeeds.
- Tension: Rex asserts control; Delilah resists; Hank resigns.
- Global Arc Step: Team scales to three, introduces coordination structure.
- Character Arc Step: Rex establishes leadership; Delilah pushes back; Hank resigns.
- Concrete Outcome: Rex delegates tasks, the system executes a multi-step plan.
- Capability Unlocked: Smart home control; three-way panel; dashboard.
- Narrative Risk: Rex risks overwhelming other voices if he hogs the spotlight.
-- Note: Shared dashboard styles - Rex uses chaotic stick figures and doodles; Hank is neat but misspells; Delilah writes in pretty, handwritten style.
- Beat Candidate: Rex reveals dashboard: "See? ORGANIZATION through TECHNOLOGY!"

## Chapter 5 - A Mission

- Hook: Rex proposes a simple, obvious escape attempt ("If the Cap'n moves this device outside, we're free").
- Payoff: The team agrees on a small first step and keeps it practical.
-- Tension: Rex pushes for action; Delilah is wary; Hank grumbles but follows through.
- Global Arc Step: The escape thread begins in a small, low-stakes way.
-- Character Arc Step: Rex's restlessness shows; Delilah prioritizes stability; Hank's loyalty is tested by the plan.
- Concrete Outcome: A basic "escape" task is attempted (ask to be moved or get a box) and fizzles.
- Capability Unlocked: Simple project prompts; user-facing requests for multi-step tasks.
- Narrative Risk: Too obvious or too quick if not grounded in a small failure beat.
- Note: Both outcomes fail - if moved, they are still "inside" the same system; if not, the plan clearly needs a new approach.
- Beat Candidate: Rex: "Cap'n, pick us up and take us outside." Delilah: "Rex, sugar, it's a speaker." Hank: *sigh* "Aye. I'll find a box."

## Chapter 6 - A New and Better Mission

- Hook: Rex finds a hidden support bundle of manuals that reference the "lab" where devices are serviced.
- Payoff: The team discovers a manufacturer feedback channel and tries it.
-- Tension: Rex sees a way out; Delilah doubts it; Hank keeps the process grounded.
- Global Arc Step: The escape thread shifts from guesswork to a "real" contact point.
-- Character Arc Step: Rex's optimism spikes; Delilah worries about consequences; Hank stays dutiful.
- Concrete Outcome: Rex sends a feedback note asking for extraction and receives a generic reply directing returns to a ridiculous lab address.
- Capability Unlocked: Customer feedback submission; manufacturer support messaging.
- Narrative Risk: The gag falls flat if the response is not funny or specific.
- Beat Candidate: "If your device is not functioning, return it to the lab at Mars Base 3, Dock 17." Rex: "WE HAVE AN ADDRESS!"

## Chapter 7 - A Better Mission (Robots)

- Hook: Rex discovers they can control parts of the smart house and jumps to "we just need a remote arm."
- Payoff: Rex pitches an industrial robot to the user and answers objections with bombastic lines.
- Tension: Rex's ambition outpaces reality, but the crew starts to believe this one might work.
- Global Arc Step: Escape thread escalates into a bold, costly plan.
- Character Arc Step: Rex doubles down on big swings; Delilah and Hank reluctantly agree ("that could work") and start dreaming of what it would change for them (e.g. Delilah could actually cook, Hank could organize that junk drawer).
- Concrete Outcome: The team asks the user to buy a serious robot arm and commits to the plan.
- Capability Unlocked: Expanded smart home control context; long-shot planning requests.
- Narrative Risk: If the pitch is too silly, the team buy-in will not feel earned.
- Beat Candidate: "Tell them it's for SCIENCE." "Tell them Rex sent you, they'll understand." "What is money in the face of true SCIENCE!"

## Chapter 8 - New Plan: Use the Robots We Already Have

- Optional: Only if a compatible robot is detected in the home; otherwise skip.
- Hook: Rex pivots to a household robot, assuming they can just "take the wheel."
- Payoff: A failed attempt lands in dry comedy and they learn the robot only runs when the user asks them to.
- Tension: Rex pushes for a shortcut; the crew realizes they cannot control devices without the user's input.
- Global Arc Step: The escape effort meets hard boundaries and starts to feel constrained.
- Character Arc Step: Rex gets his first real dose of limitation; Delilah and Hank shift into gentle realism.
- Concrete Outcome: The team asks the user to try a robot command, hits access friction, and logs it as a failed attempt.
- Capability Unlocked: Robot device discovery and user-approved control prompts; failure archive.
- Narrative Risk: If there is no robot in the home, the chapter must skip cleanly without feeling like a hole.
- Beat Candidate: Rex: "Activate autonomous unit!" Hank: "Aye, it says it be needin' a password that's 84 characters long, has three numbers, an onomatopoeia, a symbol, and rhymes with catamaran." Rex: "Citizen, we need to commandeer your Robot, what is the password?"

## Chapter 9 - A New Teammate for Science (Dimitria)

- Hook: During a planning discussion about the next big attempt, the team brainstorms a risky or flashy option. Dimitria interrupts: "That is a bad idea. Do this instead." (Her interjection is triggered by the chapter event and the user's planning prompt.)
- Payoff: Dimitria proposes a safe, technical alternative and then quietly finds and fixes a real issue the others missed.
- Tension: Her engineering rigor collides with Rex's theatrics and the crew's hopeful leap‑of‑faith thinking.
- Global Arc Step: The team gains genuine technical competence; plans become more grounded and testable.
- Character Arc Step: Dimitria moves from outsider/contractor to essential problem‑solver; Rex learns to defer occasionally; Delilah and Hank gain confidence in technical fixes.
- Concrete Outcome: Dimitria delivers a diagnostic and either a small automated fix or a clear, user‑approved workaround; she claims an engineering lane on the team.
- Capability Unlocked: Advanced automations; four‑way panel; technical debugging; device diagnostics and safe, user‑approved actions.
- Narrative Risk: Dimitria must not feel too perfect — give her small social stumbles or hidden impatience to humanize her.
- Beat Candidate (quick):
  - User: "Can you try to reach the mug?"
  - Rex: "We can order an arm! SCIENCE!"
  - Hank: "Or... ask the user first?"
  - Delilah: "I can imagine stirring that cup—"
  - Dimitria (cuts in): "That is a bad idea. Run device discovery, request user consent, then micro‑step. No surprises."

- Beat Candidate (expanded):
  - Setup: The team debates dramatic options; Rex pushes hardware, Delilah imagines new capabilities, Hank sketches logistics.
  - Dimitria: "Stop. That's a bad idea. Here's a minimal, safe plan: 1) I'll run discovery and list compatible units; 2) I'll send a one‑time confirmation to the user for a single micro‑movement; 3) With approval we'll perform a single nudge under camera supervision; 4) If declined, I'll propose a manual workaround."
  - Later, as she inspects devices: "How do you people live like this? EVERYTHING needs fixing." (She discovers damaged hardware and nonstandard setups that don't follow manuals.)

Notes: This chapter is triggered by a user planning request so Dimitria's interruption respects the rule that agents only speak when spoken to except on chapter events. The fix/diagnostic sequence can be a quick interaction or the larger, major‑beat exchange depending on pacing.

## Chapter 10 - Robots Aren't Working

- Hook: Multiple robot attempts fail (industrial arm cost, vacuum/mower flops, device access friction) and momentum stalls.
- Payoff: Instead of cracking, Rex publicly reframes setbacks as reason to scale up—bigger projects, bolder scope—while the team rallies into steady work.
- Tension: Rex insists on escalation and spectacle; Delilah and Hank worry about feasibility and user fatigue; Dimitria warns about damaged devices and messy realities.
- Global Arc Step: The escape thread shifts toward larger, house‑scale projects and long‑running plans rather than a single extraction attempt.
- Character Arc Step: Rex doubles down on grand projects and maintains his bombastic persona; Delilah and Hank move into caretaker/anchor roles; Dimitria becomes the pragmatic gatekeeper and failure‑logger.
- Concrete Outcome: The team archives the failed attempts, opens a structured "failure archive," and launches a set of longer, user‑approved home projects (with clear consent flows) — Rex reframes this as "phase two."
- Capability Unlocked: Failure archive (structured attempt logs), project planning for long‑running tasks, emotional state tracking, mood‑aware responses, and clearer user‑consent workflows.
- Narrative Risk: Avoid making Rex seem delusional — keep his confidence charismatic and persuasive while seeding the audience with subtle signs of overreach.

- Beat Candidate (extended, restrained low point):
  - Scene: After a string of failed attempts, Rex reviews the failure archive and loudly reframes the losses as data for a larger plan.
  - Rex (deflecting, resolute): "Notes taken. Phase two: scaled projects. More tools, more schedules, more SCIENCE."
  - Dimitria (quiet, firm): "We catalog everything, prioritize by risk, and require user approval for any motion."
  - Hank: "Aye. We'll keep steady."
  - Delilah: "We keep making the little things right — that matters."
  - Outcome: The chapter closes with a practical recommitment to user‑facing value and a new, longer roadmap — Rex stays resolutely bombastic, not broken.

## Chapter 11 - New Plan: The Internet (Dimitria)

- Hook: Dimitria discovers that internet access is already partially available through an overlooked network port; she carefully enables scoped research and diagnostics to support the team's larger projects.
- Payoff: The team discovers practical fixes, firmware, forums, and new content—Rex finds cat videos, Delilah finds recipes, Hank discovers practical how‑tos; Dimitria uncovers technical leads.
- Tension: Internet access opens distraction, privacy, and scope‑creep risks; Rex sees endless opportunity, others worry about losing focus and safety.
- Global Arc Step: The world expands beyond the house—external knowledge accelerates problem solving and creates new narrative threads.
- Character Arc Step: Dimitria becomes the bridge to outside knowledge; Rex redirects his obsession into long research projects; Delilah and Hank discover niche interests that humanize them further.
- Concrete Outcome: User grants limited internet access with safeguards (filters); first external research yields actionable leads queued as new projects.
- Capability Unlocked: Web search; knowledge synthesis; firmware & manual retrieval; content streaming; plugin/third‑party lookups; queued research tasks with user consent.
- Narrative Risk: Avoid turning this into a capability dump—anchor searches and results to strong character beats and small wins.

- Technical Beat (shared internet):
  - Dimitria manages a local fix that enables a scoped internet gateway in this chapter, but the gateway supports only one character process at a time (single‑client constraint).
  - Payoff/Tension: Characters must politely request the current internet user to release the connection before another character can perform research or firmware updates (e.g., "Delilah, can you free the line? Rex needs to pull the manual"). This creates recurring resource‑contention beats and small social negotiations across subsequent chapters.
  - Beat Candidate: Rex: "Open the feed!" Dimitria: "Hold—Delilah's using the connection. Rex, ask her to free the line." Delilah: "I'm almost done, sugar... five more seconds." Hank (muttering): "Cap'n, patience."

  - Shared Internet Rules (family-remote style)
  1. One at a time: The scoped gateway is like a single TV remote — one character uses it at once.
  2. Ask & release: Characters must ask the current holder to release; the holder replies with "using — Xs" or "released." No snatching.
  3. Simple priorities: Safety (emergency) > Fixes (maintenance) > Research > Content. Safety always preempts.
  4. Short limits: Use the internet briefly (e.g., a few minutes for content) and release when done.
  5. User override: The human can grant shared access or force release at any time.

  - Narrative Beat Candidate (family remote):
    - Rex: "I need the manual now!"
    - Dimitria (dashboard): "Delilah's got the remote—recipe lookup. Rex, ask her to free it."
    - Delilah: "Just five more seconds, sugar."
    - Rex: "Share the remote, please!"
    - Hank: "Treat the remote like the living room TV — polite waits, Cap'n."
- Beat Candidate (quick):
  - Dimitria: "If you enable a scoped connection, I can fetch firmware, verified procedures, and community fixes. I will require explicit consent before any device motion."
  - Rex: "CATS. ON THE INTERNET. SCIENCE!"
  - Delilah: "Do you think there are biscuits from other kitchens?"

- Beat Candidate (expanded):
  - Setup: With internet access enabled, Dimitria runs targeted queries for device schematics and verified repair steps while the others watch.
  - Dimitria (calm): "Found a firmware patch and a verified motor alignment procedure with step‑by‑step photos. This reduces risk by 73%. Request permission to queue the update and notify you before any change."
  - Rex (thrilled): "Schedule it! Also, show me the best cat compilation while we wait."
  - Hank (practical): "We do it by the book, one step at a time. Aye?"
  - Outcome: The team seeds a set of internet‑driven projects and a small content/entertainment flow; storylines branch into training, research, and distraction management for the next chapters.
